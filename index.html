Polarized light

The superposition of two circularly polarized waves with identical frequencies and amplitudes produces a plane-polarized wave.

Polarized light

When circularly polarized light passes through a refracting medium, its velocity (and therefore its wavelength) decreases, resulting in a change of phase upon exiting the medium.

Circular birefringence

If the medium has different indices of refraction for left- and right-circularly polarized light, the two waves will depart the medium out of phase, resulting in a rotation of the plane-polarized light they produce.

Circular dichroism

If the medium has different absorption coefficients for left- and right-circularly polarized light, the originally plane-polarized wave will depart the medium with elliptical polarization.

Take-home messages

  • Accurate wavefunctions for accurate properties
  • Particularly for mixed electric-magnetic properties
  • Understanding with simple models is possible
  • But we are far from having a good general model of molecular chirality
  • Solvent effects complicate the picture considerably
